### 1.1 McKinsey Global Institute (MGI) — Four Global Forces

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Organization** | McKinsey Global Institute |
| **Framework** | No Ordinary Disruption: Four Global Forces |
| **URL** | https://www.mckinsey.com/mgi/no-ordinary-disruption |
| **Published** | 2015 (foundational), continuously updated via MGI research |

**Categories / Forces:**

1. **Urbanization & Emerging Markets** — Shift of economic activity to emerging markets and cities within them; simultaneous industrial and urban revolutions shifting the center of the world economy east and south.
2. **Accelerating Technological Change** — Scope, scale, and economic impact of technology acceleration; ubiquity and speed of change.
3. **Aging Global Population** — Smaller workforce, greater productivity burden, pressure on government finances from elderly care.
4. **Greater Global Connections** — Trade, capital, people, and data flows; interconnected world economy.

**Maturity Classification:** Not formally phased; uses qualitative assessment of force momentum and disruption potential.

**Impact Measurement:** Estimated at 10x faster, 300x scale, ~3,000x impact compared to the Industrial Revolution. MGI uses economic modeling and scenario analysis.

**Strengths:**
- Simple, memorable four-force model
- Backed by deep economic data and modeling
- Strong interdependency analysis between forces

**Weaknesses:**
- No explicit maturity scoring
- High-level; lacks granular trend decomposition
- Framework dates from 2015; supplemented but not formally revised

## Part III: Tech-Specific Frameworks

### 3.1 Gartner Hype Cycle

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Organization** | Gartner |
| **Framework** | Hype Cycle (since 1995) |
| **URL** | https://www.gartner.com/en/research/methodologies/gartner-hype-cycle |

**Five Phases of the Hype Cycle:**

| Phase | Description | Key Characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Innovation Trigger** | Breakthrough, product launch, or event generates interest | No usable products; commercial viability unproven |
| 2. **Peak of Inflated Expectations** | Early publicity produces success stories, often with failures | More hype than proof of delivery |
| 3. **Trough of Disillusionment** | Interest wanes as implementations fail to deliver | Producers shake out; investment continues only if survivors improve |
| 4. **Slope of Enlightenment** | Early adopters see benefits; understanding crystallizes | Second/third-generation products emerge |
| 5. **Plateau of Productivity** | Real-world benefits demonstrated; mainstream adoption | Technology goes mainstream |

**Additional Classification Dimensions:**

- **Benefit Rating:** Transformational / High / Moderate / Low
- **Years to Mainstream Adoption:** Less than 2 years / 2-5 years / 5-10 years / More than 10 years / Obsolete before plateau
- **Market Penetration:** 1-5% / 5-20% / 20-50% / Over 50%

**Data Sources:** 2,500+ business and technology experts; 500,000+ client interactions; 27,000+ vendor briefings; 715,000+ vetted peer reviews.

**Strengths:**
- Most widely recognized technology maturity model
- Multiple Hype Cycles per year covering different domains (AI, security, IoT, etc.)
- Multi-dimensional classification (phase + benefit + time-to-mainstream)
- Massive data infrastructure

**Weaknesses:**
- Criticized for poor predictive accuracy (few technologies actually follow the full cycle)
- Over-simplifies complex adoption patterns
- Proprietary/commercial access
- Subjective analyst placement on curve
- Y-axis ("expectations") is abstract and unmeasurable

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Organization** | ThoughtWorks |
| **Framework** | Technology Radar (biannual since 2010) |
| **URL** | https://www.thoughtworks.com/radar |

**Four Quadrants (Technology Categories):**

2. **Tools** — Software tools for development, testing, deployment
3. **Platforms** — Infrastructure, cloud, mobile, and compute platforms
4. **Languages & Frameworks** — Programming languages and application frameworks

**Four Rings (Recommendation Levels):**

| Ring | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Adopt** | Proven, mature technology; industry should adopt | Use with confidence |

**Strengths:**
- Actionable recommendation levels (Adopt/Trial/Assess/Hold)
- Practitioner-driven (working technologists, not analysts)
- Free and publicly accessible
- Biannual updates capture rapid change
- Opinionated and specific

**Weaknesses:**
- Software engineering focus only (no business, societal, or geopolitical trends)
- Subjective expert opinion (22 people)
- No quantitative scoring
- ThoughtWorks-centric perspective

### 6.2 Futures Studies Methodologies

#### 6.2.1 Delphi Method

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | RAND Corporation, 1950s |
| **Purpose** | Structured expert consensus forecasting |

**Process:**
1. Select expert panel (typically 15-60 experts)
2. Round 1: Individual forecasts on specific questions
3. Statistical aggregation and anonymized feedback
4. Round 2-3: Revised forecasts after seeing group distribution
5. Convergence toward consensus (or documented dissensus)

**Output:** Probability estimates, time horizons, and impact assessments for specific developments.

#### 6.2.2 Causal Layered Analysis (CLA)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Creator** | Sohail Inayatullah (1998) |
| **Purpose** | Deep structural analysis of issues across four layers |

**Four Layers:**

| Layer | Question | Type of Knowledge |
|---|---|---|
| **Litany** | What is happening? | Observable events, quantitative data |
| **Systemic Causes** | What are the structures causing this? | Economic, political, social systems |
| **Worldview** | What deep assumptions underlie this? | Cultural, civilizational perspectives |
| **Myth/Metaphor** | What is the foundational narrative? | Deep stories, archetypes, collective unconscious |

#### 6.2.3 Scenario Planning

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | Herman Kahn (RAND), Pierre Wack (Shell), Peter Schwartz (GBN) |
| **Purpose** | Exploring multiple plausible futures |

**Standard Approach:**
1. Identify focal question
2. Identify key driving forces
3. Rank by importance and uncertainty
4. Select two critical uncertainties as axes
5. Develop 2x2 scenario matrix (4 scenarios)
6. Flesh out scenario narratives
7. Identify implications and early indicators

**Strengths of Futures Studies Methods:**
- Deep structural analysis (CLA)
- Uncertainty management (scenarios)
- Expert knowledge aggregation (Delphi)
- Well-established academic foundation

**Weaknesses:**
- Labor-intensive
- No standardized scoring
- Scenario quality depends on facilitator
- Delphi convergence can mask valid minority views

### 7.4 Impact Scoring Methodology (1-100)

Multi-criteria impact scoring synthesized from WEF (likelihood x impact), Gartner (benefit rating), Forrester (three-axis evaluation), and futures studies assessment methods.

**Five Impact Dimensions (weighted):**

| Dimension | Weight | Score 1-20 | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | 25% | How broadly does the trend affect sectors, geographies, and populations? | 1-4: Niche sector/region; 5-8: Multiple sectors or regions; 9-12: Cross-sector or multi-regional; 13-16: Pan-industry or global; 17-20: Universal transformation |
| **Depth** | 25% | How fundamentally does the trend change existing structures, behaviors, or systems? | 1-4: Incremental improvement; 5-8: Significant enhancement; 9-12: Structural change; 13-16: Paradigm shift; 17-20: Civilizational transformation |
| **Irreversibility** | 20% | Once adopted, how difficult is it to reverse or undo the trend? | 1-4: Easily reversible; 5-8: Reversible with effort; 9-12: Difficult to reverse; 13-16: Practically irreversible; 17-20: Permanently irreversible |
| **Speed of Impact** | 15% | How quickly does the trend produce tangible effects once it reaches adoption? | 1-4: Decades; 5-8: 5-10 years; 9-12: 2-5 years; 13-16: 6-24 months; 17-20: Immediate/disruptive |
| **Second-Order Effects** | 15% | How many downstream cascading effects does the trend generate? | 1-4: Minimal spillover; 5-8: Some adjacent effects; 9-12: Significant cascading; 13-16: Major systemic effects; 17-20: Cascading across all dimensions |

**Composite Impact Score:** `(Scope * 0.25) + (Depth * 0.25) + (Irreversibility * 0.20) + (Speed * 0.15) + (Second-Order * 0.15) = Score (1-20, then multiply by 5 for 1-100 scale)`

**Impact Categories:**

| Range | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1-20 | **Marginal** | Limited, localized impact |
| 21-40 | **Moderate** | Meaningful impact within specific domains |
| 41-60 | **Significant** | Cross-domain impact with structural implications |
| 61-80 | **Transformative** | Reshapes major systems and behaviors |
| 81-100 | **Civilizational** | Fundamentally alters human civilization |

### 7.5 Confidence Scoring Methodology

Based on IPCC calibrated uncertainty language, Delphi consensus measurement, and intelligence community analytical standards.

| Score | Label | Definition | Evidence Requirements |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1-20 | **Speculative** | Based on single signals, unverified claims, or theoretical possibility only | 1-2 weak signals; no expert consensus; no data |
| 21-40 | **Low Confidence** | Some supporting evidence but significant gaps. Expert opinions diverge. | Multiple signals but contradictory; minority expert view; limited data |
| 41-60 | **Medium Confidence** | Reasonable evidence from multiple sources. Moderate expert agreement. Key uncertainties remain. | Several independent data sources; majority expert agreement; some quantitative support |
| 61-80 | **High Confidence** | Strong evidence from diverse sources. Broad expert consensus. Limited remaining uncertainties. | Multiple quantitative datasets; strong expert consensus; historical precedent |
| 81-100 | **Very High Confidence** | Overwhelming, convergent evidence. Near-universal expert agreement. Trend already observable in data. | Convergent multi-source evidence; near-universal expert agreement; directly measurable |

**Confidence Modifiers:**
- **+10** if corroborated by 3+ independent framework sources (e.g., Gartner + Deloitte + McKinsey all identify the trend)
- **+5** if quantitative data supports the trend (patent data, funding data, adoption metrics)
- **-10** if only a single source identifies the trend
- **-5** if significant expert disagreement exists
- **-10** if trend is primarily hype-driven with no deployment evidence

**Velocity Score (1-10):**

|---|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | **Dormant** | No measurable movement | Stagnant funding, declining media mentions, no new entrants |
| 3-4 | **Slow** | Gradual, linear progression | Steady but modest funding growth, incremental adoption, regular publications |

### 7.10 Implementation Recommendations

**For the Strategic Intelligence System:**

1. **Adopt the four-level hierarchy** (Mega > Macro > Micro > Signal) as the primary organizational structure. This is the most widely validated pattern across TRENDONE, Gartner, and futures studies literature.

2. **Use STEEP+V as the dimensional backbone.** Six dimensions are manageable yet comprehensive. Every major framework maps cleanly to these categories. The "V" (Values) dimension captures cultural and ethical shifts that STEEP alone misses.

3. **Implement the 6-phase maturity model.** It harmonizes Gartner's well-known phases with Perez's installation/deployment lens and ThoughtWorks' action-oriented rings.

4. **Deploy the five-factor Impact Score (1-100).** Weighted multi-criteria scoring is more robust than single-axis assessments. The five factors (Scope, Depth, Irreversibility, Speed, Second-Order Effects) capture what no single framework does alone.

5. **Use calibrated Confidence Scoring** modeled on IPCC language standards. Cross-framework corroboration should be the primary confidence booster.

6. **Track Velocity through quantitative signals** (funding, patents, media, jobs, regulation, publications, adoption surveys). This is the biggest gap in existing frameworks and the highest-value addition.

7. **Maintain five Time Horizons** to serve different strategic decision contexts from operational to generational.

8. **Build cross-cutting linkages.** Following PwC and Zukunftsinstitut, emphasize interactions between trends. A trend's impact is often amplified by its connections to other trends across dimensions.

9. **Source triangulation.** For each trend, track which frameworks identify it. Trends identified by 3+ independent frameworks merit higher confidence. Trends identified by only one source remain signals until corroborated.

10. **Continuous update cadence:**
    - Signals: Weekly scan
    - Micro-Trends: Monthly review
    - Macro-Trends: Quarterly assessment
    - Mega-Trends: Annual deep review
